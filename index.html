The Author

Rayann Tedds

Computing student at Coventry University

Inspiration

For decades, science, technology, engineering and mathematics (STEM) have been perceived as ‘male’ subjects.

It is a recurring problem through many influential countries in the world and this is reflected by the under-representation of women in STEM in education and careers.

As a young woman, I took an interest in what are commonly seen as ‘male’ subjects and I have experienced working in a male-dominated industry where I felt the pressures and lack of support in STEM.

At the age of 12, I experienced a male maths teacher expressing his views that I was not good enough to be within the top-ability group which lead to a massive knock in my confidence - it was only when another teacher expressed their faith in me and supported me that I really began to thrive.

The aim of this app is to provide young women with inspiration, advice and support to help stimulate an interest in STEM.

Marie Curie
Science
Most remembered for her discovery of radium and polonium, as well as her help with the fight against cancer
  • Researched into radioactivity
  • In 1903, she won the Nobel Prize for Physics
  • She gained a doctorate in Physics
  • In 1911, she won the Nobel Prize for Chemistry for her work with finding a way to measure radioactivity.
  • By October 1914, she had developed small X-ray units 'Petits Curies' which were used to diagnose battlefield injuries.
  • Following the war, she won other awards including:
    • Ellan Richards Research Prize (1921)
    • Grand Prix du Marquis d'Argenteuil (1923)
    • Cameron Prize from Edinburgh University (1931)
  • Marie Curie charity is set up in her name to support cancer patients.
Rosalind Franklyn
Science
Most remembered for her work which aided the understanding of the DNA, RNA, viruses, coal and graphite molecular structures.
  • Her X-ray diffraction images of the DNA molecule played a key part in the discovery of the double helix model.
  • Her work advanced the knowledge of polio and tobaccoo mosaic viruses and their molecular structure.
  • She published her findings and research in papers throughout the years.
  • In 1942, she accepted a fellowship to the British Coal Utilisation Research Association.
  • In 1958, she received a 3 year grant from the U.S. National Institute of Health to continue her research work.
Lise Meitner
Science
Most remembered for being in the team that discovered nuclear fission.
  • Researched into radioactivity
  • In 1905, at the University of Vienna, she became the second lady to earn a PhD in Physics.
  • She was the first ever female allowed to attend Max Planck's lectures
  • In 1909, she published 2 papers on beta radiation.
  • In 1926 she became the first woman in Germany to become a full professor of Physics.
  • In 1945, Otto Hahn received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry but Meitner's was not named as a co-recipient due to gender bias.
  • She won awards and other achievements including:
    • Woman of the Year by the National Press Club (1946)
    • Max Planck Medal of the German Physics Society (1949)
    • Otto Hahn Prize of the German Chemical Society (1955)
  • Meitnerium (element 109) is named after her.
Gertrude B. Elion
Medicine
Most remembered for her work in drug research and development.
  • At the age of 15, she graduated with the highest honors in Chemistry.
  • In 1988, she won the Nobel Prize for Medicine.
  • She was a leading American chemst and the holder of 45 patents.
  • She discovered the drug acyclovir which is used for the treatment of herpes.
  • She discovered the drug Pyrimethamine which is used for the treatment of malaria.
  • She also advanced the field of medicine when it came to other illnesses such as leukemia, AIDS and gout.
  • She won awards and other achievements including:
    • Garvan-Olin Medal (1968)
    • National Medal of Science (1991)
    • Lemelson-MIT Lifetime Achievement Award (1997)
  • She is deemed to be the first women named in the National Inventors Hall of Fame in Akron.
Ada Lovelace
Computing and Mathematics
Most remembered for her work and research on the theories of 'jumping genes'.
  • In 1923, she recieved a Bachelor's degree in Botany and went on to further recieve a Master's and a PhD.
  • She became the first scientist to understand the nature of chromosomes during meisosis, as well as diagramming it.
  • She also conducted researching into X-rays and their mutuating properties.
  • She was the first scientist to fully understand the centromere and its role in genetics, as well as outlining it to others.
  • In terms of achievements and awards, she faced lots of sexual discrimination meaning her work contributions were not recognised.

Brittany Wenger
Teen Computer Whiz
Developed an 'artificial brain' technology aimed at diagnosing breast cancer.
  • She designed an artificial brain to detect signs of breast cancer, after a family member was diagnosed with the disease.
  • She was only 19 years old at the time.
  • This breast cancer test now has its own application and is being trialled in a few cancer research centers.
  • The test has a 99% accuracy rating.
  • In 2013, she was named one of Time Magazine's 30 under 30.
